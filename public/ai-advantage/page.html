02 · Phase 1

The 5-Layer Model

Map the problem before asking for solutions. The breakthrough always happens at Layer 3.

1
Symptom — What you notice
The thing that shows up as a problem. Real — but not the problem itself.
Sarah's answer
"Compliance paperwork is eating my Monday mornings."
Prompt for this layer
Layer 1
"The biggest problem I keep running into is [describe it]. It affects [who] and happens [how often]. Don't solve it yet — ask me questions to understand it better."
2
Process — What's actually happening
Map the steps. Who does what, when, using which tools. Look for handoffs and gaps.
Sarah's answer
"Certificates tracked in a spreadsheet. Inductions logged manually. Expiries not flagged anywhere."
Prompt for this layer
Layer 2
"I'm going to describe how [this process] works, step by step. After I'm done, map it back as a numbered sequence and flag where you see manual steps, handoffs, or gaps."
3
Constraint — Where it breaks down Key Layer ★
The specific point where it fails. Name this precisely — the solution designs itself.
Sarah's answer
"No single owner. Whoever is around does it. Records live in three different places."
Prompt for this layer
Layer 3 ★
"I think the real bottleneck is [your best guess]. Push back on that — is that really the root constraint? Restate it in one sentence I can act on."
4
Cost — What not solving it costs
Vague cost = low urgency = no action. Quantify in time, money or risk.
Sarah's answer
"2 hours every Monday. Two near-misses last year. If we get audited this month — we fail."
Prompt for this layer
Layer 4
"This costs me roughly [estimate] in [time/money/risk] per [week/month]. Help me be precise — ask about costs I might be missing, then summarise the total impact."
5
Owner — Why it hasn't been solved
Who has the problem and why does it persist? This shapes what solution will actually stick.
Sarah's answer
"Technically me — but the solution needs to run without me chasing it every week."
Prompt for this layer
Layer 5
"This problem has existed for [timeframe]. The reason it hasn't been solved is [honest assessment]. What does a solution need to look like to actually get used — not just built?"
The test at every layer
"If this was solved — what would actually change?"
If the answer is vague, you're still at the symptom. Keep going.

Phase 1 Master Prompt

Phase 1 Master Prompt — screenshot this
I want to unpack a problem in my business. Don't suggest solutions yet. Work through these 5 layers with me, one at a time. Ask me ONE question per layer, wait for my answer, then move to the next. After all 5, restate the real problem in 2 sentences I would agree are exactly right. Layer 1 - SYMPTOM: Ask me what problem I keep running into and who it affects. Layer 2 - PROCESS: Ask me to describe what actually happens, step by step. Layer 3 - CONSTRAINT: Ask me where it breaks down and why it keeps happening. Layer 4 - COST: Ask me what it costs in time, money or risk — be specific. Layer 5 - OWNER: Ask me who should own this and why it hasn't been fixed yet. Start with Layer 1 now.
03 · Phase 2

The C³ Method

Three moves. Used together, they produce results neither you nor AI would reach alone.

C¹Clarity
Load the context
"I'm a [role]. Here's my problem..."
"Here's the current process step by step..."
"The bottleneck is..."
"The tools we already use are..."
C²Curiosity
Let AI investigate
"Before answering, ask me questions one at a time"
"What am I not thinking about?"
"What would an expert do differently?"
"Give me 3 options and explain the trade-offs"
C³Challenge
Push back — don't settle
"What are the risks or edge cases?"
"Rate this out of 10. Explain why. Now make it a 10."
"What would you change if starting from scratch?"
"Poke holes in this. What's missing?"

Phase 2 Prompt — send immediately after Phase 1

Phase 2 Prompt
Good. Before you suggest any solutions — Summarise back to me what you've understood about my problem so far. Then ask me anything you're still not sure about, one question at a time. Once you feel confident you understand it — restate the real problem in 2 sentences I'd agree are exactly right. Only then ask me if you can start suggesting solutions.

Plan Prompt — send once the solution is agreed

Plan Prompt
Good. We've agreed on the solution. Now help me build a step-by-step implementation plan. Give me: 1. The agreed solution in one sentence. 2. The steps to implement it — in order, with who does what. 3. What done looks like — specific and measurable in 30 days. 4. The first action I can take this week. Keep it practical. I need to be able to hand this to someone on Monday.

Stress-test prompts

"Rate this out of 10. What would make it a 10?"
"What are you assuming that might not be true?"
"If I could only build one thing first — which and why?"
"What would make this fail?"
"What have I not asked that I should have?"
"What's the simplest version that still solves this?"
04 · The Example

Sarah's story.

Every framework demonstrated through a real problem. Here's the full arc.

Her roleOffice manager. Trades business. 25 staff, subcontractors rotating in and out.
Her problemCompliance. Certificates expiring. Inductions not logged. Spreadsheet nobody trusts.
Her weekEvery Monday morning — two hours — before she'd started anything else. Three years.
Her attemptsTried to fix it twice. Both times it collapsed. Back to the spreadsheet both times.

The C³ conversation

C¹ ClaritySarah loads context before asking for anything
AI
I'm an office manager at a trades business — 25 staff, subcontractors in and out.

Compliance is a constant headache. No single owner, records in three places, certificates expire without warning.

Before you suggest anything, ask me questions one at a time until you understand what's actually going wrong.
Role + context loaded. Constraint named upfront. Sarah hands control to AI — not asking for a solution, asking AI to investigate.
C² CuriosityAI leads. Sarah answers — doesn't jump to solutions.
AI
Before I suggest anything — when a certificate lapses without anyone catching it, what actually happens? Does the worker stop? Does a supervisor step in? Or does it quietly slip through?
Honestly? It slips through. We've had two subcontractors on site with expired white cards in the last six months. We didn't know until an audit.
That's important. Second question: when someone is responsible for checking certificates, who is it — and do they have a reliable way to see expiry dates, or are they manually checking each person?
AI is building a picture — not generating solutions. Each question goes one level deeper.
⚠ The TrapAI gives an answer. Confident — but wrong. Most people stop here.
AI
Based on what you've told me:

1. Create a shared Google Sheet with all certificates and expiry dates, colour-coded by status.
2. Set up Google Calendar alerts 30 days before each expiry.
3. Assign one person as 'compliance owner.'
Confident. Not correct. Sarah has tried the Google Sheet twice. It works for a month then collapses. Only she can spot this — which is exactly why Challenge matters.
C³ ChallengeSarah pushes back. AI changes direction entirely.
AI
We've tried the Google Sheet twice. It works for a month then nobody maintains it. The problem isn't the tool — it's that compliance gets deprioritised the moment anyone is busy. What am I not thinking about?
That's a critical distinction — thank you for pushing back.

If manual maintenance always fails under pressure, the system can't rely on human remembering. Is there a point in your existing workflow — onboarding, site sign-in, invoicing — where compliance status is already checked? Because the answer is to embed it there, not add a separate process.
One push back. AI changed direction entirely. The real solution is now reachable.
AI restates — Sarah confirms
"The real problem is that compliance checking only happens when someone remembers — and under pressure, nobody does. A new tool won't fix that. The solution must be automatic and embedded in an existing workflow."
"Yes. That's exactly it. That's what I've been trying to say for three years."

A better checklist. Something to replace the spreadsheet.
She needed
A system that doesn't depend on anyone remembering.

Compliance embedded into site sign-in — automatic, no maintenance burden.
Monday mornings: 2 hours → 20 minutes.
